WIP: How AI is affecting the workforce: are ethnic minorities more vulnerable?

A closer look at AI usage amongst professions and populations, based on Anthropic's Economic Index

By Lucia de la Torre

The rise of artificial intelligence threatens to dramatically reshape the American workforce, with some ethnic groups potentially facing more significant economic challenges than others. Based on Anthropic's Economic Index and Bureau of Labor Statistics data, a stark picture emerges of how technological disruption could unequally impact different communities across the United States.

Recent data from Anthropic's Economic Index reveals significant insights into how AI is being used across different occupations and income brackets. Computer and mathematical occupations lead with 37.2% of AI-related queries, followed by arts and media (10.3%) and education (9.3%), showing a concentration in mid-to-high wage professions.

The data reveals a troubling pattern when overlaid with ethnic workforce distribution. In occupational categories with high AI usage, there are notable differences in representation between white and non-white workers. While certain occupations with high AI integration like Computer and Mathematical fields show relatively better diversity, other sectors show significant ethnic imbalances, suggesting that AI's benefits and disruptions will not be distributed equally.

Dr. Maya Rodriguez, Labor Economist at the Center for Future Work, offers perspective on this emerging divide: "We're standing at a critical juncture where technological innovation intersects with long-standing economic inequalities. The AI revolution isn't just a technological challenge—it's a profound test of our commitment to inclusive economic opportunity."

This income-based analysis reveals that AI usage varies significantly across earnings brackets, with medium-high income levels showing stronger AI adoption. However, the ethnic composition within these brackets varies as well, with white workers representing between 71% and 76% of the workforce across income levels. The lowest representation of white workers (71%) appears in the Medium-Low income bracket, suggesting that certain non-white workers may face both technological disruption and wage disadvantages simultaneously.

Preliminary research suggests that Latino, Black, and certain Asian American workers may be disproportionately employed in occupations most susceptible to AI-driven automation. These roles span critical sectors including administrative support, customer service, and manufacturing—areas where machine learning and generative AI technologies are rapidly advancing.

The intersection of race, income levels, and potential AI impact creates a complex landscape of economic uncertainty. As Rodriguez explains, "These technologies can either reinforce existing inequalities or help bridge them—the outcome depends entirely on how we design, deploy, and regulate AI systems with equity in mind."

While these findings provide critical insights, they come with methodological limitations. The datasets rely on self-reported racial and ethnic identification, which can be nuanced. Moreover, AI's potential impact depends on technological development, workplace integration, and regulatory responses that remain uncertain.

As AI continues to evolve and reshape work across the economy, proactive policy interventions, targeted workforce retraining programs, and equitable technology implementation will be essential in ensuring that the benefits of AI are broadly shared and that vulnerable workers are not left behind during this transition.
